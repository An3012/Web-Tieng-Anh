In the US, an estimated 30 million people — 1 in 8 Americans over the age of 12 — have hearing loss in both ears.

Millions would benefit from hearing aids but most have never tried them, according to the National Institute on Deafness.

Many other people have tried hearing aids, but don't use them because of cost, poor quality, how they look or for other reasons.

Over the past few years, there has been a push to change that. Two years ago, federal rules changed to allow hearing aids to be sold over-the-counter, a move that many hoped would bring better and cheaper options to patients.

And in September, the US Food and Drug Administration approved software from Apple that would turn AirPods into hearing aids.

The hearing aid feature is available with the Pro 2 models, and requires an iPhone or iPad to set it up. It starts with built-in tests that will help users determine if they have hearing loss, set up the feature if they do, and set personalized amplification levels.

The headphones sell for $249 on Apple's website, and sometimes less from other retailers.

While there are many other earbud-type devices that can function the same way, experts agree that the AirPods addition is a good one, because of the way it could help normalize the wearing of hearing aids.

The ubiquity of AirPods could make people who are worried about the look of a hearing aid more open to using them.

There may be some drawbacks, though. Fit and comfort during long use might be an issue. Other hearing aids are built to last through a whole day or more, the battery life of the AirPods won't allow for that.

Wearing earbuds may also make others think that the person doesn't want to be disturbed or spoken to.
